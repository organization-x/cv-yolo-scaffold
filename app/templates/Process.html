Pothole Detector by AI

Process

We initially started out brainstorming the concept of machine learning, how it can detect the potholes on the roads. Although, it have a high accuracy of detecting potholes, but there are still a percentage of errors displaying. Whereas, the confusion matrix can help classify each errors.

Data Collection
When making an AI, the most important thing to perfect is your data. When using a supervised learning model, you need to label all the data so you can have a desired output for the neural network to work off of. For example, in our case, if there was a pothole in an image and our model did not detect any, the weights would need to be adjusted to get the desired output. Even when using a model with pretrained weights, a large amount of data, labeled photos of potholes in our case, is required for training. 

Splitting up the Data
After gathering and labeling all the data we could, we ended up with around three thousand labeled images. The next step was to split the data into a training, testing, and validation set. The training sets would be used to train our model, testing sets were used to test our model to see if it was functioning as intended, and validation sets that would be used as images for fine tuning our model's parameters. For our model, we sectioned off 80% of images to training, 10% to testing, and the final 10% to validation. 

Formatting the data
 After we had our training, testing, and validation sets figured out it was time to change the format of the data we had collected. Because we had collected our data from a number of different sources, we had data that were incompatible with our model.  After coding in various functions to clean up the files to our desired file types, we could then begin running our model.

Model Training
To begin the training process, we used a NVIDIA Tesla T4 GPU with 16 GB of VRAM and began feeding in our data. After around 12 hours it was done and ready to test. When testing photos manually, it seemed to work seamlessly, however, our validatation dataset had other things to say about it. This is when we began to get mixed messages about the performance and really started using confusion matrices to dive deeper into our problem. 

Inherent Problems
After diving deeper into the issue, the variation in labeling techniques looked to be the cause. As we had a multitude of people labeling the data, there was certain biases toward what counted as a pothole and what didn't. Because of this fact, images with many small potholes are bound to not get labeled. To combat this, we had people reviewing back on previously labeled data to make a more uniform dataset with minimal bias in it. After further review, the new data was fed into our model and we arrived at a much better model.
